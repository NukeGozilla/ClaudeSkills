## 1. Positioning and the "one thing" exercise

Before planning any calendar, get clarity on the user's positioning. Most social accounts flounder because they're trying to be interesting about everything. The accounts that grow consistently are known for *one thing*.

The "one thing" exercise:
- Finish this sentence with the user: "If someone discovers my account today, within 3 posts they should know I'm the person who [___]."
- Good answers are specific and ownable: "writes about B2B pricing strategy from actual deal experience" / "teaches sourdough bread to beginners with a chemistry background" / "covers AI tooling for lawyers."
- Bad answers are vague or over-broad: "talks about business" / "shares marketing tips" / "inspires people."

The "one thing" isn't a cage — users can and should range within it. But the center of gravity stays fixed, and new followers understand what they're signing up for.

If the user doesn't know their "one thing" yet, that's step zero. Offer to help them find it through audience research + a quick audit of their best-performing existing content.

## 2. Content pillars

Content pillars are 3-5 recurring themes the user rotates through. They're the tactical implementation of "the one thing."

Good pillars are:
- **Specific** (not "tips" but "pricing teardowns of real SaaS products")
- **Repeatable** (the user won't run out of material in two weeks)
- **Distinct from each other** (so the feed feels varied)
- **Connected to the positioning** (each pillar makes the "one thing" clearer)

### Example: Pillars for a B2B SaaS founder

1. **Deal teardowns** — specific wins, losses, and what changed between them
2. **Pricing & packaging** — what we tried, what worked, what didn't
3. **Team/hiring lessons** — hard-earned stuff about building the first 20
4. **Industry takes** — observations on where the market is going, opinionated
5. **Personal moments** — the occasional off-topic post that reminds people there's a human behind the account

### Example: Pillars for a baking creator

1. **Technique teardowns** — one technique per video, clearly explained
2. **Recipe adaptations** — "what happens if I swap X for Y"
3. **Kitchen disasters** — failure videos, which perform well and build trust
4. **Pantry science** — the why behind what works
5. **Reader challenges** — respond to reader questions or disasters

### Pillar ratios

Don't treat pillars evenly. Usually one pillar is the heaviest hitter (maybe 40-50% of output), others are supporting (20% each), and the "personal" pillar is ~10%. Ratios shift over time based on what performs.

## 3. Cadence — how often is enough?

The right cadence is "the most the user can sustain for 6+ months without burning out or dropping quality." That number is almost always lower than social media advice suggests.

### Baseline realistic cadences (by platform, for most users)

- **LinkedIn:** 2-5 posts/week. More than daily risks saturation for the same audience.
- **X:** 1-5 original posts/day + active replies. The reply volume matters more than original-post volume for growth.
- **Instagram:** 3-5 Reels/week + daily Stories. Grid posts 1-3x/week if doing them at all.
- **TikTok:** Daily if growing aggressively; 3-5x/week is the sustainable floor for an active account.
- **YouTube (long form):** 1/week is standard; 2/week is ambitious; <1/week is fine if quality is high.
- **YouTube Shorts:** 3-7/week for active growth.
- **Facebook:** 3-5x/week on Pages; whenever in Groups.
- **Threads:** 1-5x/day if porting an IG audience; less if starting fresh.

### The consistency principle

Posting 3x/week every week for 6 months beats posting 7x/week for 4 weeks then stopping. The algorithms and audiences both reward consistency over bursts. Plan for a sustainable rhythm, not an aspirational one.

### Multi-platform cadence

Most users shouldn't try to be everywhere. Two platforms done well > five done badly. Typical combinations that work:
- LinkedIn + X (for B2B, founders, operators)
- Instagram + TikTok (for consumer, creator, lifestyle)
- YouTube + one short-form (Shorts or TikTok) to feed it
- Threads + Instagram (same audience, parallel content rhythms)

## 4. Content calendars that people actually follow

A content calendar fails if it's overly precise or overly vague. The sweet spot: specific themes per slot, flexible specifics within.

### A lightweight calendar template (weekly view)

Prefer this to a rigid Monday-Tuesday-Wednesday grid:

- **Pillar-1 post, twice a week** (e.g., deal teardown Monday + Thursday)
- **Pillar-2 post, once a week**
- **Pillar-3 post, once a week**
- **Reactive/topical slot, once a week** — leave open for timely takes
- **Rest day** — genuinely no post. Protect this.

Put it in a shared doc or spreadsheet. For a more structured user, a 4-6 week view with draft titles is better than rigid day-of-week assignments.

### The backlog

Good calendars are built from a backlog, not by staring at a blank doc each week. Keep a running list of post ideas (titles, hooks, half-drafts) and pull from it. Refill the backlog opportunistically — when the user has a spare 30 minutes, don't draft one post; capture 10 ideas.

### When to batch

Batching drafts (writing 5-10 posts in one session) works for some users and breaks others. It works well when:
- The user has limited weekly availability and needs to protect content time
- The content pillars lend themselves to parallel drafting (e.g., a series)

It breaks when:
- The user's best content is reactive to news/events
- The user's voice is strongest when in-the-moment

Default recommendation: batch the evergreen pillars; leave one reactive slot per week for in-the-moment posts.

## 5. Campaign and launch playbook

When a user has a specific moment — product launch, book launch, event, funding announcement, fundraise, new role — social needs to work harder for a defined window.

### Phase 1: Seed (4-6 weeks out)

Plant the narrative without announcing. Three posts that tease the theme:
- A post about *the problem* the launch solves (no product mention)
- A post about *the journey* ("I've been working on something for 8 months...")
- A post *asking* an audience question that maps to the product

### Phase 2: Build (2-3 weeks out)

Generate anticipation. Three to five posts:
- A reveal (blurred mockup, waitlist link, countdown)
- Behind-the-scenes (screenshot, process, team)
- One piece of longer-form content (blog/newsletter) that the social posts can point to
- A "why I'm doing this" founder post on the core platform
- Cross-platform adaptation of each of the above

### Phase 3: Launch (day of + 48h)

High-volume, varied. Five to eight posts:
- The main announcement post (your flagship format)
- Screenshots or demo video
- Customer/user testimonial (if available)
- The origin story
- A "here's what's new and different"
- A clear CTA post
- A thread deep-dive on one feature
- A Reels/Shorts demo

### Phase 4: Sustain (2-4 weeks after)

Don't let the launch disappear. Three to four posts:
- Early results / metrics (if impressive)
- A customer win
- An answer to a common question
- A "lessons from launch" reflection post

Total: ~20-30 pieces of content across 8-10 weeks, not 50. Quality and coordination matter more than volume.

## 6. Audience research and persona-building

Good content speaks to someone specific. The easiest way to avoid generic output is to draft for one named person.

### The "10 real people" exercise

Ask the user to list 10 real people they'd love to reach — by name when possible, by clearly specified archetype otherwise ("Series A founder at a dev tools startup who just hired their first marketer"). For each:
- What problem are they trying to solve?
- What are they already reading, watching, or following?
- What language do they use for their problem (often different from jargon)?
- What would make them save or share a post?

The common patterns across those 10 are the user's real audience. Draft for those patterns, not for "my followers."

### Persona pitfalls

- **Fake personas with invented demographics.** "Marketing Mary, 34, likes yoga" is useless; it doesn't help you write better posts.
- **Aspirational audiences that don't match current reality.** A 2k-follower creator targeting "Fortune 500 CMOs" will flop. Start with the adjacent layer — people 1-2 steps up from the current audience.
- **Assuming the audience in the analytics is the audience you should serve.** Sometimes they're misaligned — e.g., the user wants to reach founders but 70% of their engagement is marketers. Decide whether to lean in or pivot.

## 7. The compounding content system

The difference between users who plateau and users who compound is that compounders recycle and connect.

### Recycle

- **Repost your best stuff.** A post that worked 6 months ago can be reframed and reposted. Your audience has mostly forgotten, and new followers haven't seen it. Don't just copy-paste — refresh the hook.
- **Spin one idea into multiple formats.** A podcast becomes: a YouTube video, 3 Reels clips, a LinkedIn carousel, a thread, an Instagram caption, a Threads post. One hour of source material = 2 weeks of content.
- **Turn comments and DMs into posts.** If 3 people ask the same question, that's a post.

### Connect

- **Reference earlier posts.** "Last week I said X. Here's the update." Builds a body of work rather than disconnected posts.
- **Build serials.** Weekly "Sunday review," monthly "what I built this month," quarterly "lessons from…" create appointment-viewing.
- **Cross-reference formats.** A post can point to a YouTube video; a YouTube video can reference a newsletter; a newsletter can call back to a thread.

## 8. Red flags in a social strategy

When reviewing a user's existing strategy or plan, watch for these:

- **"Post every day" without a pillar structure.** Will run out of ideas in 2 weeks.
- **"Go viral" as a goal.** Viral is an outcome, not a strategy. Ask what actually matters — followers, reach, leads, community, sales — and plan for that.
- **Spreading across 5+ platforms.** Almost always wrong for individuals and small teams. Cut to two.
- **Posts optimized for algorithm games at the expense of positioning.** Using a trending sound that has nothing to do with the user's expertise will get reach without audience quality.
- **No feedback loop.** A strategy with no plan to look at what worked is hope, not strategy. Build a 30-day review cadence in.
- **No "opinion" posts.** If every post is tutorial or takeaway, the user becomes a utility, not a person. Utilities don't build brands.
- **Too much behind-the-scenes, too little value.** BTS content is seasoning, not the meal.
