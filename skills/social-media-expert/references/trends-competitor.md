## 1. How to research a trend without chasing it cringely

The difference between a brand that rides a trend well and one that rides it embarrassingly is whether the trend *fits*. A B2B pricing expert jumping on a dance trend is cringe; the same person using a trending audio as ironic background for a pricing teardown can work.

### The 3-question trend filter

Before jumping on a trend, answer:

1. **Does the trend's format serve the user's idea?** (If the trend is "green screen with a shocked reaction," does the user have an idea that suits that format?)
2. **Will the trend's audience overlap with the user's ICP?** (A dance trend's audience is broad; a "what's in my bag" trend on founder Twitter is more targeted.)
3. **Can the user execute it authentically?** (If attempting it feels forced, it reads as forced.)

If the answer to any of these is "no," skip it. Most trends should be skipped.

### Trend windows

Trends have lifecycles. Late is usually worse than never:
- **Emerging (days 1-5):** High-reach if you can ship fast. Often the creators who caught it early get the bulk of the reach.
- **Peak (days 5-14):** Still works if your take is differentiated, but competition is high.
- **Saturated (days 14+):** Usually avoid. Jumping on a 3-week-old trend screams "trying to be relevant."

Exception: trends that become enduring formats (POV, Story Time, "tell me X without telling me X") are fair game anytime.

### Signals that a trend is worth riding

- Creators in the user's niche (not just general TikTok creators) are doing it.
- The format lends itself to a take, not just a performance.
- The user can ship within 48-72 hours of spotting it.

## 2. Competitor audit framework

When the user asks "what are my competitors doing?" or "who should I be watching?", here's the structured way to answer.

### Step 1: Identify the right competitors

- **Direct competitors:** Other accounts selling/serving the same audience with similar offerings.
- **Aspirational competitors:** Accounts 2-3 levels bigger, same niche — shows what the ceiling looks like.
- **Adjacent competitors:** Accounts serving the same audience with different offerings — shows what audience also engages with.

Identify 3-5 in each bucket. Write them down. Don't stop at the obvious names.

### Step 2: For each competitor, pull:

- **Posting cadence** — how many posts per week per platform
- **Pillar mix** — what percentage of content is what type (skim their last 20 posts and tag them)
- **Hook patterns** — read their top 10 performing posts; identify the 3 most common hook shapes they use
- **Engagement rate** — likes+comments divided by follower count (rough, but useful)
- **Top performers** — specifically what are their top 5 posts of the last 6 months? What do they have in common?
- **Weak spots** — what do they NOT do well? (often a better source of opportunity than their strengths)

### Step 3: Synthesize into "what to borrow, what to avoid, what to do differently"

Good audits produce a short doc, not a data dump:
- **Borrow:** formats and cadences that are working and fit the user's voice
- **Avoid:** things the competitor does that feel off-brand or low-quality
- **Differentiate:** gaps in the competitive landscape the user can own

This is the actionable output. Spreadsheets of competitor data without this synthesis are useless.

### Audit frequency

Do a deep audit quarterly. In between, a light check-in (5 minutes per competitor, look at top posts from the last month) monthly is plenty. Weekly is obsessive and counterproductive.

## 3. Hashtag and keyword research

Different platforms work differently here. Do platform-appropriate research.

### Instagram & TikTok

- Search the candidate hashtag natively. Look at:
  - Post volume (too small = no discovery; too big = buried)
  - Recency (is "Recent" populating? If not, the tag may be restricted)
  - Content type match (are the top posts in this tag the kind of content the user makes?)
- Use a tool if available (Keywords Explorer, Hashtagify, RiteTag); otherwise native search is enough.

### LinkedIn

- Search the hashtag in LinkedIn's search. Look at follower count (LinkedIn shows how many people follow a hashtag).
- 10k-500k followers is the sweet spot for most B2B hashtags. More than 1M is usually too noisy.

### X

- Hashtag research on X is less useful in 2025 than it was. Community tags (#buildinpublic, #growthhacking, #devtools) still have active users; most others are dead. Search natively to see if there's recent, real activity.

### YouTube (keyword, not hashtag)

- Use YouTube search autocomplete to find real queries people type.
- Use TubeBuddy, vidIQ, or keyword planners to estimate search volume.
- Target queries where the top results are older, smaller channels, or missing something — that's the gap.

## 4. Spotting viral patterns

When the user asks "why did this go viral?" or "what's working right now?", break it down structurally:

### The 5 dimensions of a viral post

1. **Hook** — what made people stop? (Specific, curious, controversial, visual)
2. **Structure** — what made people stay? (Short paragraphs, payoff timing, loop, thread structure)
3. **Emotional beat** — what did they feel? (Recognition, surprise, validation, anger, awe, envy, relief)
4. **Shareability trigger** — why did people pass it on? (Sound smart, signal identity, help a friend, settle an argument)
5. **Timing/context** — was it riding a news cycle, a trend, a moment?

Break down 3-5 viral posts in the user's niche using this framework. The pattern across them reveals the current "physics" of the niche.

### What NOT to copy from viral posts

- **Exact topics.** If 5 people are writing "I got laid off, here's what I learned," the 6th is late and derivative.
- **Exact wording.** Viral posts get detected as recycled fast.
- **Hooks that don't match the user's actual experience.** "I made $1M in 30 days" from someone who hasn't made $1M reads as fraud.

### What to extract

- The underlying structural pattern (hook shape, payoff timing, emotional beat)
- The latent reader need (what did the viral post give them that they're hungry for?)
- The gap (what would the *next* evolution of this look like?)

## 5. When the user doesn't have access to analytics tools

Sometimes the user just wants a read on what's working without buying a SaaS tool. Here's what you can do with free/native access:

- **Native platform analytics** (LinkedIn insights, X analytics, Instagram Insights, TikTok analytics, YouTube Studio) — free, surprisingly good, often underused. Start here.
- **Google Trends** for topic/keyword interest over time.
- **Reddit + niche Slack/Discord communities** — where the target audience actually complains about things. A goldmine for hook material.
- **Competitor's top posts (sorted by engagement)** — most platforms let you sort a user's posts by top. This is free trend research.
- **Save-for-later signals** — on IG and TikTok, saves are a strong signal; check which of the user's past posts had high save rates. Those topics are content goldmines.

## 6. Where the live data actually lives

Live trend data can't be fetched from memory — the skill needs to be honest about that. If the user asks "what's trending today?", the right move is:

- Tell them you can't see live trend data from inside the skill itself.
- If they can share screenshots, a trending sounds page, a Reddit thread, a competitor's recent post — analyze what they share.
- If they want live browsing, suggest using the web tools available in the parent session (search, fetch) to pull current trend pages from TikTok Creative Center, Instagram Explore, X trending topics, YouTube trending, Reddit r/popular + relevant subreddits, Google Trends.

Don't fabricate trends. "Right now #_________ is trending" without data is worse than saying you don't have real-time access. Users can tell when you're inventing.
