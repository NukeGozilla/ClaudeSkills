## 1. The hook — why it's 80% of the work

On every scroll-based platform, the first line or the first 2 seconds of video decide whether the post gets read at all. The algorithm sees someone scrolled past → kills distribution. The algorithm sees dwell time → boosts distribution. Everything else in the post is downstream of the hook working.

Practically, this means:
- Spend disproportionate time on the first line. If you draft a post in 10 minutes, 4 of those should be on the hook.
- Draft 3-5 hooks, not one. Pick the sharpest. Treat the rejected hooks as starting points for other posts.
- The hook promises something — curiosity, a number, a contrarian take, a story. Whatever it promises, the rest of the post must deliver.

A weak hook with great content still underperforms a strong hook with mediocre content. That's unfair but it's the game.

## 2. Hook patterns that work

A non-exhaustive library. Use these as starting scaffolds, not templates to fill in robotically. The best hooks feel personal and specific — these are just the shapes.

**The specific failure:**
> "I fired my highest-performing salesperson last quarter. It saved the company."

Works because: specific action + counterintuitive outcome + promise of the explanation.

**The counterintuitive claim:**
> "Most founders should not do content marketing."

Works because: violates the dominant narrative; forces a double-take.

**The specific number:**
> "We spent $47,000 on a rebrand. Nothing changed."

Works because: specificity makes it believable; size of number creates stakes.

**The numbered list preview:**
> "7 things I'd do differently if I started my company again. #4 would have saved us $200k."

Works because: clear value promise + curiosity gap on the payoff.

**The mini-scene/dialogue:**
> "My co-founder, yesterday: 'We need to fire half the team.' / Me: 'I know.'"

Works because: cinematic opening; creates a "what happens next?" pull.

**The earned observation:**
> "After 847 sales calls this year, here's the one question that closes every deal."

Works because: credibility through volume + specific payoff.

**The public prediction:**
> "AI video will eat YouTube Shorts in the next 18 months. Here's why."

Works because: bold stake + implicit reasoning promise.

**The outsider observation:**
> "I just spent a week in Tokyo. American startups are doing one thing completely wrong."

Works because: external vantage point creates implicit authority.

**The micro-before/after:**
> "Wrote this on my iPad in 40 minutes. It did $12k in sales. Here's what I learned."

Works because: specific inputs + specific outputs + learning promise.

**Hooks to avoid (overused, feel AI-generated):**
- "Here's a hot take..."
- "Let me tell you a story..."
- "Unpopular opinion:"
- "I don't usually post about this, but..."
- "The truth nobody is telling you about X..."

These are so worn out they read as parody. If you *do* use one, subvert it immediately.

## 3. Post structures by format

### Short post (single-tweet, short caption, Threads post)

Structure: **Hook → Supporting beat → Payoff.**

One idea, nothing more. If the idea has two parts, it's a thread.

Example (X):
> I've had 3 co-founders walk away from my companies. Each one taught me the same lesson: I was optimizing for speed when I should have been optimizing for trust.

### Medium-length post (LinkedIn, long IG caption, Facebook)

Structure: **Hook (line 1) → Setup (2-3 short paragraphs) → Turn (the insight or punchline) → Takeaway (optional CTA or question).**

Use short paragraphs — 1-2 sentences each. White space does heavy lifting for readability.

Example skeleton:

```
[Hook line — 1 line, specific, under 12 words]

[Context paragraph — 2-3 sentences of what happened]

[Turn/insight — 1-2 sentences of what you learned or noticed]

[Elaboration — 3-5 sentences developing the insight]

[Takeaway — 1 line, generalizable but not preachy]

[Optional question or CTA]
```

### Thread (X, Threads)

Structure: **Hook tweet → Promise → Body (one idea per tweet) → Payoff tweet → CTA tweet.**

- Hook tweet: sets up what you'll cover and why it matters. Does not give away the payoff.
- Body tweets (usually 5-10): one idea each. If a tweet has two ideas, split it. Short sentences, white space, occasionally a "screenshot within the thread" moment (a bolded takeaway in quotes).
- Payoff tweet: the thing the hook promised, delivered cleanly.
- CTA tweet: "If this was useful, follow @handle for more." Or "Retweet the first tweet to help this reach someone." Or link to deeper content. Don't skip this — it's how threads actually grow accounts.

### Carousel (Instagram, LinkedIn documents)

Structure: **Cover slide → One idea per slide → Final slide with CTA.**

- Cover slide: the hook. One punchy line + visual. This IS the thumbnail.
- Slides 2-N-1: one clear idea per slide. Use ~15-40 words of text per slide + a simple visual. Don't cram.
- Final slide: recap + CTA ("Save this for later" / "Share with someone building" / "DM me for the template").

Golden rule for carousels: the first slide makes people swipe, and **each subsequent slide's goal is to make them swipe again**. If a slide is boring, they leave.

### Video hook (Reels, Shorts, TikTok)

Structure: **Visual pattern interrupt (0-1s) → Verbal hook + on-screen text (1-3s) → Payoff setup (3-5s) → Body → Final reveal/CTA.**

- First second: motion, unexpected cut, or bold on-screen text — something that breaks the scroll.
- Seconds 1-3: state the payoff promise clearly. "3 things I'd never do as a small business owner" tells the viewer exactly what they'll get.
- Rest of the video: deliver. Short sentences, snappy cuts.
- Ending: the biggest payoff, or a clear CTA, or a loop back to the opening (rewatch rate is a huge ranking signal on TikTok).

## 4. Caption formulas

Captions sit below videos or images and carry the weight on IG, Facebook, and sometimes YouTube. Different formulas for different goals:

**PAS (Problem-Agitate-Solve):**
> P: "Can't figure out why your Reels aren't getting views?" / A: "You've probably tried trending sounds, hashtags, posting times. Still flat." / S: "It's almost always the hook. Here's the framework I use."

**AIDA (Attention-Interest-Desire-Action):**
> A: Hook. / I: Why it matters. / D: Paint the outcome. / A: One clear CTA.

**Before / After / Bridge:**
> Before: "You're posting 5x a week and still not growing." / After: "Your ideal clients finding you without you chasing them." / Bridge: "Stop optimizing for the algorithm. Start optimizing for one person."

**The story caption:**
> Short scene → turn → takeaway. Good for personal brands and creators.

**The listicle caption:**
> Promise ("5 things…") → numbered list, 1 line each → recap/CTA.

## 5. Hashtag strategy (cross-platform)

Platform-specific guidance is in `platforms.md`. Universal principles:

- **Relevance > volume.** A hashtag with 20k posts where your content matches beats one with 20M where you get buried in 10 seconds.
- **Mix breadth tiers.** For any platform that still rewards hashtags (IG, TikTok, LinkedIn), mix 1 broad (10M+ posts), 2-4 medium (100k-1M), 2-3 niche (<100k). This gives you reach across discovery levels.
- **Check for shadowbanned/banned tags.** Search the tag natively — if "Recent" doesn't populate, it's flagged. Skip it.
- **Don't invent brand hashtags nobody will use.** #YourStartupName2025 is a graveyard. Only invent hashtags if you're running a campaign that will generate real volume.

## 6. CTAs that don't sound like CTAs

A good CTA tells the reader what to do AND why it's in their interest. "Subscribe" is a command. "Save this for your next product launch — you'll thank yourself" is a reason.

Working CTA shapes:
- **Practical save:** "Save this — you'll want it the next time you're writing hooks."
- **Share-to-help:** "Share with the founder friend who's been stuck on this."
- **Invitation to reply:** "What's worked for you? I'm genuinely curious." (only if you'll actually read and reply)
- **Follow with reason:** "I share one post a day on B2B sales. If that's useful, follow."
- **Link with value promise:** "The full framework (with examples) is in the link below ↓"

CTAs to avoid:
- "If you liked this, smash that like button!"
- "Tag a friend who needs to see this!" (when the post doesn't really merit it)
- "Thoughts?" with no setup
- "DM me for more info!" (feels like a trap)

## 7. Editing pass: the last-minute tightening

Before handing off a draft, run this quick pass:

1. **Cut the first sentence.** Often you'll find the *second* sentence is the real hook. If so, swap them.
2. **Delete any adverb you can live without.** "Really," "very," "genuinely," "actually," "truly." Usually removable.
3. **Replace every generic word with a specific one.** "Our team" → "three of us." "A lot" → "$2.1M." "Some customers" → "Acme and Globex."
4. **Shorten every sentence you can.** If a sentence has two "and"s, it's probably two sentences.
5. **Check the last line.** It should land. If it dribbles, cut it or rewrite.

## 8. Common drafting mistakes

These keep showing up; watch for them.

**Bullet-dumping.** A wall of bullets in a LinkedIn post looks lazy. Convert to full sentences with white space.

**Over-explaining the metaphor.** If you make an analogy, trust the reader to get it. "It's like building a house — you can't start with the roof (meaning, you need fundamentals before tactics)" — the parenthetical kills it.

**Too many hooks.** One post, one hook. If your draft has 3 punchy lines competing for the first slot, you have three posts.

**Preamble before the hook.** "So the other day I was thinking…" / "Okay, this is going to sound weird, but…" — cut it. Start at the hook.

**Generic endings.** "Just my thoughts!" / "Anyway…" / "Hope this helps!" — the post should end on the payoff or a genuine CTA, not a sign-off.

**Inconsistent pronouns.** Starting in "I," drifting into "we," ending in "you" — pick a point of view and hold it.

**Voice drift.** If the user's voice is punchy and casual, don't suddenly drop a buttoned-up corporate sentence in the middle. Re-read with their voice in your head before finalizing.
